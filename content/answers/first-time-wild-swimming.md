# What should I expect on my first Wild Swimming experience?

## Quick Answer

Expect an invigorating dip in a natural body of water, be it a lake, river, or the sea. Your first time should be a carefully planned and safe experience, focusing on acclimatization to the cold and understanding the environment. Consider going with an experienced guide or group for your initial swims.

## Preparing for Your First Dip

Wild swimming in Wales offers stunning locations, but preparation is key. Operators like TYF Adventure in Pembrokeshire offer guided wild swimming experiences from around £60, which are ideal for beginners. These sessions typically include a safety briefing, guidance on acclimatization, and the use of appropriate equipment like wetsuits and tow floats. Alternatively, if you're going solo, start with easily accessible spots like Llyn Padarn in Eryri (Snowdonia), where the entry is gentle and the water is relatively calm. However, be aware that Llyn Padarn can get busy, especially during peak season (July-August), so arrive early for parking. Water temperatures vary drastically depending on the time of year, ranging from a chilly 5°C in winter to a more manageable 15°C in summer.

## Understanding the Welsh Waters

Welsh waters present unique challenges. Coastal swims, while exhilarating, require understanding tides and currents. Always check tide times before entering the sea. The waters around Anglesey, for example, can have strong currents. Inland, be mindful of water quality, especially after heavy rainfall, which can increase pollution levels in rivers. Websites like Natural Resources Wales provide water quality information. For a more controlled environment, consider swimming in designated bathing waters like those at Barafundle Bay, known for its clear waters and sandy beach. Remember that even in summer, Welsh weather can be unpredictable, so pack layers and be prepared for changing conditions.

## Practical Tips

- Always check the weather forecast and water conditions before you go; microclimates can change rapidly in Wales.
- Wear a brightly coloured swim cap and consider a tow float to increase your visibility to other water users.
- Park responsibly. Many popular swimming spots are in rural areas with limited parking; avoid blocking access for locals and emergency vehicles.
- Acclimatize gradually. Don't jump straight into the water; enter slowly to allow your body to adjust to the cold.
- Never swim alone, especially in unfamiliar locations.
